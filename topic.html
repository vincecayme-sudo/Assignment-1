How do Digital Twins work?

Digital twins are essentially computer programs that use real-world data to create simulations. — TWI Global.

Mathematicians and data scientists research the physics and current and/or historical operational data of a physical object or system in order to create a simulated model of said object. Digital twins may utilise artificial intelligence, software analytics and sensors that transfer live data from the original to allow the user to measure performance, visualise and predict current and future events.

An example would be creating a digital twin for a space launch. As the rocket is exiting earth’s atmosphere and well into space, the scientists are able to monitor and adjust fuel ratios, thrust, trajectory, etc. in correspondence with the data received in real-time.

Types of Digital Twins

1. Component Twin (small part)

A component twin is a digital copy of one small part of something. It focuses only on that single piece, not the whole object. The purpose is to monitor and understand how that one part is working in real time. They are digital models of individual components or parts such as motors, sensors, switches and valves.

Example:

  • A twin of a single Network Switch or specific Database Server.
  • Car engine sensor. In real life, the sensor measures temperature. On the computer, the digital twin shows the same temperature live. If the sensor gets too hot, the system knows immediately.

Summary: Only that one small part is copied and tracked.

2. Asset Twin (whole object)

When more than one component works together, they form an asset. This twin represents a complete piece of equipment. It is a digital copy of a complete object. Instead of just one part, it includes all parts of that object working together as one unit.

Example:

  • A twin of an entire Server Rack or cloud, where hardware, OS and storage interact.
  • A full car. The digital twin shows speed, fuel level, engine condition, etc. Everything about the car is shown in one system.

Summary: The entire object is copied and monitored.

3. System Twin (many things together)

A system twin is made up of multiple assets and is a digital copy of multiple objects connected all together. It shows how different parts or machines work together as a system. They are digital models of entire systems or processes. They allow organizations to analyse a system performance area where improvements can be made.

Example:

A shopping centre. It has lifts, lights, air conditioning, and security systems. The digital twin shows how all these work together at the same time.

Summary: It focuses on connection between many things.

4. Process Twin

A process twin is a digital copy of a process (a series of steps over time). It focuses on how something happens from start to finish. They are digital models of entire business processes or customer journeys.

Example:

  • A twin of DevOps Pipeline or Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)
  • An online order.
    1. Order placed
    2. Packed
    3. Shipped
    4. Delivered

The digital twin shows each step happening live.

Summary: It focuses on steps and flow, not just objects.

Benefits of Digital Twins

  1. Predicting and Preventing Problems

    By constantly analysing data, digital twin can spot early warning signs of equipment failure before a breakdown actually happens. This can help to reduce maintenance costs and unexpected failures.

    For example: F1 teams like Mercedes or Red Bull have digital twins of their race cars. Thousands of sensors on the real car send data back to the factory. If the virtual twin shows the engine is likely to overheat in three laps, the engineers tell the driver to change settings immediately to prevent a "DNF" (Did Not Finish).

  2. Risk Free Experiments

    Digital twins allow you to perform experiments that would be too dangerous or expensive in the real world. It’s an "Undo" button for reality. You can crash a virtual plane, flood a virtual city, or overheat a virtual nuclear reactor to see what happens, without anyone getting hurt or losing a penny.

    For example: Designers can test new products in virtual environments to ensure they are safe and reliable before they are ever built. A factory wants to see if a new, faster robot arm will accidentally hit a human worker. Instead of building the floor and hoping for the best, they run the simulation 5,000 times in the digital twin. If the virtual robot hits a virtual human, they just change the code and try again.

  3. Improving Safety and Public Welfare

    They can forecast the paths of hurricanes or the spread of wildfires, allowing authorities to take action accordingly. Digital twins help us "test" the future. By simulating how a city handles a disaster, we can find the best escape routes or the strongest ways to build.

    For example: They help engineers monitor the stress on bridges and dams to ensure they can survive severe weather. The city of Singapore has a complete digital twin ("Virtual Singapore"). They use it to simulate how a massive flash flood would flow through the streets. This helps them decide exactly where to build drainage systems and where to place emergency barriers to save lives.

  4. Faster Innovation

    Companies can build and test a thousand virtual prototypes for a fraction of the cost of building one physical version. It removes the waiting time. In the past, you had to build a prototype, test it, find a flaw, and build a new one.

    For example: Instead of sewing a thousand different shoes to see which sole is the springiest, Nike can create digital twins of sneakers. They can virtually "run" in them using different materials to see which one lasts the longest before they ever cut a single piece of fabric.

History of Digital Twins

Apollo 13 (1970)

An early real-world example of the digital twin concept can be seen in the Apollo 13 mission (1970). According to NASA, engineers maintained highly accurate ground-based replicas and simulations of spacecraft systems.

When an oxygen tank exploded during the mission, these ground systems allowed engineers to replicate the failure conditions and test solutions before communicating them to the astronauts.

NASA explains that this approach demonstrated how maintaining a mirrored version of a system on Earth could support real-time problem solving and mission safety, even when the physical system is far away in space.

This event is widely referenced as a precursor to modern digital twin technology because it showed the value of mirroring physical systems to improve decision-making.

The Concept: Dr. Michael Grieves (2002)

The formal concept of the digital twin was introduced in 2002 by Dr. Michael Grieves during a presentation at the University of Michigan to the Society of Manufacturing Engineers (SME).

At the time, he referred to it as the “Information Mirroring Model.” His work focused on Product Lifecycle Management (PLM), which involves managing a product from its design phase through to its disposal.

According to documented sources, Grieves defined three key components:

  • Physical Product:
    the real-world object
  • Virtual Representation
    the digital version of that object
  • Data Connections
    the flow of data between the physical and virtual systems

He also emphasised that the digital representation should act as a central source of information throughout the product’s lifecycle, enabling better decision-making and reduced reliance on physical testing.

The Coining of the Term (2010)

In 2010, the term “Digital Twin” was formally introduced by John Vickers of NASA. This occurred as part of NASA’s efforts to improve modelling and simulation for complex systems.

NASA required a way to monitor and manage “unreachable assets”, such as spacecraft, satellites, and rovers. These systems cannot be physically accessed once deployed, so engineers rely on digital models combined with sensor data and historical records.

According to NASA’s technical reports, digital twins were proposed as a way to integrate:

  • Real-time sensor data
  • Historical maintenance data
  • Physics-based simulation models

This allowed for predictive analysis and risk assessment, making digital twins a critical tool for mission planning and operations.

Industrial Scaling (2010–2018)

During the 2010s, digital twin technology expanded into industry, particularly in manufacturing and energy sectors.

Companies such as GE and Siemens adopted digital twins to model equipment like turbines, engines, and production systems. According to GE, digital twins enable organisations to simulate performance, predict failures, and optimise operations.

A major factor in this expansion was the growth of the Internet of Things (IoT). By around 2018, sensors could be embedded into machines, allowing continuous real-time data collection.

This transformed digital twins into dynamic systems that update continuously, improving accuracy and enabling predictive maintenance.

The Modern Era: Smart Cities and Medicine (2019–Present)

In recent years, digital twin technology has expanded to large-scale and human-focused applications.

Smart Cities

According to industry and academic sources, entire cities can now be digitally modelled. These digital twins allow planners to simulate infrastructure changes, traffic systems, and environmental impacts before implementing them in the real world.

Medical Twins

Healthcare applications include creating digital models of organs or patients using imaging technologies such as MRI. These models help doctors analyse how a patient may respond to treatments, improving decision-making and personalisation.

Industrial Metaverse

Recent developments (2024–2026), such as those highlighted by Siemens and Dassault Systemes, combine digital twins with AR and VR technologies. This allows engineers to interact with digital systems in immersive environments and collaborate remotely.

Research institutions like the University of Melbourne are also contributing to digital twin development, particularly in urban systems and infrastructure modelling.
